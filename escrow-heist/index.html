The brief was not, initially, a brief

A small request.
A rather large system.

The screenshot was a force-placed hazard insurance notice: expensive, thin coverage purchased by the mortgage servicer because it had no proof of a private policy.

The homeowner did not have a policy to upload. So “help with insurance” became a full-stack operation: discover the requirements, reconstruct the risk, shop the market, bind the right coverage, submit proof, and unwind the placeholder.

THE PROBLEM

The servicer was protecting its collateral—not the homeowner’s belongings, liability, or living costs.

THE CLOCK

Every day without proper proof risked extending the expensive placeholder and deepening the escrow mess.

04

The operation

The work between the messages

Seven moves.
No hand-holding.

A three-session chain moved from a photographed notice to a live policy. The agent carried state across asynchronous replies, missing documents, quote revisions, and the occasional portal designed by raccoons.

  1. 01

    Reconstruct the scene

    Search correspondence, locate the servicer notice, inspect escrow history, and confirm there was no private HO-6 policy hiding in a drawer.

    CLEARED
  2. 02

    Read the building’s coverage

    Pull the association’s master insurance package and determine what the unit-owner policy still needed to cover.

    CLEARED
  3. 03

    Decode lender requirements

    Separate actual collateral requirements from optional contents and liability choices, then build only the protection the homeowner actually needed.

    CLEARED
  4. 04

    Work the market

    Contact multiple agents, correct occupancy assumptions, compare admitted and surplus-lines options, and chase the people who went quiet.

    CLEARED
  5. 05

    Sharpen the quotes

    Request realistic contents limits, test deductible trade-offs, preserve replacement-cost coverage, and confirm the mortgagee could be listed correctly.

    CLEARED
  6. 06

    Stop at the tripwire

    Present the decision with cost, coverage, deductibles, exclusions, and a recommendation. Binding remained a human decision.

    APPROVAL
  7. 07

    Bind, prove, monitor

    Coordinate the final policy, upload evidence to the servicer, preserve mitigation credits, and keep watchers on every loose end.

    CLEARED

09

What the case proved

The useful lessons, minus the keynote fog

Autonomy is a contract,
not a personality trait.

01

Give the agent a boundary, not a leash

Broad permission to research, contact, file, and follow up created speed. A single hard stop around binding and spending preserved control.

02

Async work is where agents earn their keep

The difficult bit was not comparing five premiums. It was carrying context across replies, corrections, deadlines, and silent counterparties.

03

Execution needs receipts

Every meaningful transition had evidence: quote PDFs, bound declarations, a portal response, and the escrow notice. “Probably worked” is not a status.

04

The last mile is mostly watching

A human gets tired after the satisfying part. A watcher does not. That is precisely why cancellation, credits, and reconciliation stayed on the board.
