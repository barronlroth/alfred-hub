What It All Means

Key Takeaways

Here's what the numbers actually mean in plain English.

Calcified Plaque Is the "Better" Kind

There are two main types of arterial plaque: soft (vulnerable) and calcified (stable). Soft plaque is more dangerous because it can rupture and cause a heart attack. Mark's plaque is primarily calcified — it's hardened, stable, and significantly less likely to rupture. Think of it as a scar that's healed over versus an open wound.

CAD-RADS 2 — Mild, Non-Obstructive Disease

CAD-RADS is a standardized scoring system from 0 (no disease) to 5 (total occlusion). A score of 2 means there's visible plaque narrowing the arteries by 25–49%, but nothing is close to blocking blood flow. This is the medical equivalent of "we can see something, and we should manage it, but it's not causing problems right now."

The LAD & the "Widow-Maker" Label

The LAD artery is sometimes called the "widow-maker" because a sudden blockage there can be fatal — it supplies blood to the largest area of the heart. Mark has 87% of his calcium in the LAD, which sounds alarming, but context matters: the plaque is calcified (stable), the stenosis is mild (25–49%), and there is no obstruction. This needs monitoring and management, not panic.

What the Calcium Score Means at Age 63

A calcium score of 266 at age 63 falls around the 60th–75th percentile for men his age. That means roughly 25–40% of men his age have a higher score. It's elevated and warrants active management (statins, lifestyle optimization), but it's well within the range of "treatable and manageable." If this score were in a 45-year-old, it would be much more concerning.

Dilated Aortic Root: 4.1 cm

The aortic root (where the aorta connects to the heart) measures 4.1 cm, which is above the normal range of 2.0–3.7 cm. This is borderline dilated — not an emergency, but it should be monitored with periodic imaging (typically an echocardiogram every 1–2 years). If it were above 5.0 cm, intervention would be considered. At 4.1 cm, the priority is tracking whether it's stable or slowly growing.

Two Major Arteries Are Completely Clear

The Right Coronary Artery (RCA) and the Left Main show zero plaque and zero calcium. The RCA is the dominant vessel supplying the back of the heart, and the Left Main is the trunk that feeds the entire left side. Having these clean is genuinely good news — the disease is localized, not widespread.

Recommended Next Steps

  • Statin therapy — discuss with your cardiologist. Statins can slow plaque progression and stabilize existing calcified plaque.
  • Risk factor management — blood pressure control, cholesterol optimization, regular exercise, and a heart-healthy diet (Mediterranean-style).
  • Follow-up imaging for aortic root — echocardiogram in 12 months to monitor the 4.1 cm measurement and confirm stability.
  • Consider non-atherosclerotic causes if symptoms persist — the mild stenosis found may not explain chest symptoms, and other causes should be evaluated.
  • Lifestyle optimization — regular cardio exercise, stress management, smoking cessation (if applicable), and maintaining a healthy weight all reduce cardiac risk.
